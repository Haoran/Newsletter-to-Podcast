First up, Google researchers have uncovered the first known instance of AI-powered malware being used in a real-world cyberattack. This discovery highlights a new frontier in cybersecurity threats, as hackers begin to leverage generative AI to enhance their attacks. Google's Threat Intelligence Group identified two new malware strains, PromptFlux and PromptSteal, which can adapt their behavior mid-attack using large language models. PromptFlux is still in development, showing signs of frequent updates to evade detection. Meanwhile, PromptSteal has been used by Russian military hackers in attacks on Ukrainian entities. Though these strains are still early in their development, they represent a significant shift in the cybersecurity landscape. Google's team is particularly concerned about PromptSteal's reliance on open-source models, which could make sophisticated attacks accessible to less skilled hackers. However, most cybercriminals continue to rely on traditional methods like phishing and stolen credentials. As Billy Leonard from Google's Threat Intelligence Group puts it, while these developments are concerning, they are not cause for panic.

Next, Google is integrating its Gemini chatbot into Maps, allowing users to interact with their navigation app through conversation. With the new AI features, users can enjoy hands-free navigation on Android, iOS, Android Auto, and eventually Apple's CarPlay. This conversational system aims to make navigation more intuitive by helping users with tasks like identifying unmarked turns, reporting accidents, and checking parking availability.
